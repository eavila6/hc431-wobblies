Who’re the wild, wooley Wobblies?

The Industrial Workers of the World (IWW) formed in 1905 is an important early 20th century American union predominantly characterized by their revolutionary beliefs for the time. As a result, the members of the IWW, the Wobblies, were always a disruption to the status quo and the plans of their fellow unions— such as the American Federation of Labor (AFL), one of the largest union organizations at the time— as they fought for workers’ rights largely due to the innovative strategies they employed.

Unlike other unions, the Wobblies were all-inclusive and targeted the “workers neglected by the mainstream” (Dubofsky p.481). The Wobblies understood the importance and role of worker solidarity when it came to making a push for work-life improvement. All workers from varying skill levels, ethnic backgrounds, skin tone, etc. were welcomed into the Wobbly ranks with open arms. Despite the times they fell short at making all of their members’ voices heard— a rather difficult crease to iron out due to union workers representing minorities generally being few and far between during this time period and the Wobblies did sometimes lose themselves in the heat of the moment— the Wobblies’ open arms is one of their best traits. It’s as a result of their willingness to provide a platform and a microphone for the marginalized that makes the Wobblies so important in the history of labor.

The Wobblies were also feared not because they brought about revolutionary ideas wherever they went but rather because of their fierce reputation as unrefined brawlers. The Wobbly’s main enemy was the man in power. This was on local, federal, and even national scales since Wobblies wholeheartedly believed capitalism was an ultimate evil propped up by the ones who benefitted from it most. As a result, the Wobblies were willing to employ a variety of methods other unions avoided, ranging from the mild (random sabotage) to the extreme (tactical sieges).

Though in the moment the bloodshed begotten from these actions was horrendous, it ultimately allows for a stronger, more successful strike. Most of the Wobblies’ most “successful” protests can be identified with some sort of violent act: Free-Speech fights from which the IWW spawned(prior to 1900), Oregon Packing Company(1913), and the Everett Massacre(1916) are some examples. Despite the flawed nature of the execution of these events as well as their goryness, the Wobblies serve as an example that it’s possible to hold one's ground.
